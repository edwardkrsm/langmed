Phonological devices
PhonoRhyme
A pattern of identity of sound between words/verse-lines extending from the end to the last fully accented vowel and not further. Creates mnemonic effect.
"Eukanuba gives their teeth the strength they need."
PhonoRhythm
A systematic arrangement of sounds using prosodic features — intonation, rhythm, lexical stress. Makes text memorable subconsciously.
"Coke really rocks!" / "drinka pinta milka day"
PhonoAlliteration
Successive stressed syllables begin with the same consonant sound. Stop consonants (p,b,m,n,t,d,k,g) are most commonly used.
"Performance. Prestige. Passion for Innovation." / "Where coffee meets kindness."
PhonoAssonance
Repetition of vowel sounds within words in close proximity. Creates a musical, memorable quality.
"The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain."
Graphic features
GraphicUnpredictable spelling
Non-standard or creative spelling to create novelty and grab attention.
"Beanz Meanz Heinz" · "4ever" · "Bar B Q" · "süper"
GraphicAcronyms / initials
Letters of abbreviation highlighted by colour, size, or layout to create the first letters of product words.
"XTROVERT. XPLOSIVE. LOVE THE COLOUR. COLOR XXL"
Lexical features
LexicalNeologism
Creation of a completely new word that emerges into everyday use. Different from euphemism (which softens meaning).
"Cookability" · "Wonderfuel" · "Siplicity" (sip + simplicity)
LexicalForeign words
Used to emphasise the origin OR exclusiveness of a product in relation to a particular country.
"La crème de la crème of lipcolour."
LexicalIntertextuality
A text echoes or refers to another text — literary, biblical, film, song, idiom, historical phrase, proverb, fairy tale.
"iThink, therefore iMac" (Descartes) · "Not all those who snack are lost" (Tolkien)
LexicalHomophones
Words that SOUND THE SAME but are SPELLED DIFFERENTLY. Exploited to create puns in print advertising.
"Sole mates for your feet" (sole/soul) · "Every kiss begins with Kay" (K/Kay)
LexicalHomonymy
Two words with the SAME spelling AND pronunciation but DIFFERENT, UNRELATED meanings.
"seal" = sea animal OR wax stamp · "fair" = market OR just/equal
LexicalPun
A play on words exploiting multiple meanings (via homonymy or polysemy). Increases humour and meaning density.
"Coke refreshes you like no other can." (can = ability / drink container)
LexicalWeasel words
Words that suggest a comparison/claim WITHOUT being specific. Open comparatives ("Better") or bogus superlatives ("Best") — never state better/best than WHAT.
"Brown's Boots Are Better" · "helps", "virtually", "enriched", "guaranteed"
LexicalAmbiguity
Deliberately USED in ads (not avoided) to make a phrase memorable and re-readable. Can be syntactic or semantic.
"Flying planes can be dangerous." (syntactic) · Puns (semantic)
LexicalCollocations
Familiar word combinations, often slightly deviated to create a pun and link product to a known phrase.
"Do you believe in love at first touch?" (deviation of "love at first sight")
Grammatical / structural features
GrammarImperative
Command verb with no explicit subject — creates direct personal address; all ads urge action. Three verb groups: acquisition (buy/get/choose), consumption (try/have/use), notice (look/see/watch).
"Just do it." · "Taste the rainbow." · "Don't just wear it. Feel it."
GrammarPresent tense
Most common tense in ads — conveys timelessness and universality. Simple PAST is used for storytelling and nostalgia effects.
Present: "It refreshes." / Past (nostalgia): "Remember when summer meant..."
GrammarParallelism
Repetition of formal syntactic patterns — presents two or more similar ideas in the same structural form. Creates rhythm and emphasis.
"Stay dry, stay happy." · "Plant one, grow many."
GrammarAnaphora
Same word or phrase repeated at the BEGINNING of consecutive clauses/sentences.
"Explore the hills. Explore the rivers. Explore the mountains." · "Good hair. Good mood. Good day."
GrammarEpiphora
Same word or phrase repeated at the END of successive clauses/sentences.
"See new. Hear new. Feel new." (the word "new" at the end of each clause)
GrammarEllipsis
Omission of part of a grammatical structure. Saves space, creates informality, intimacy, and immediacy.
"Expecting guests?" = "(Are you) expecting guests?" · "Sleep like nature intended [you to]."
Grammar2nd person pronouns
"You / your" — used to ADDRESS the audience directly and suggest a friendly, familiar attitude. NOT first person.
"Your skin's new best friend." · "We know what you really want."
Semantic / rhetorical devices
SemanticPersonification
An inanimate object or abstract concept is spoken of as if it were a living entity with human attributes (sensations, emotions, powers of speech).
"They will stay on the job longer than most employees." (Volvo tyres) · "Where coffee meets kindness."
SemanticSimile
A direct, expressed comparison between two unlike things using "like", "than", "as", or "as if".
"Sleep like nature intended." · "Breakfast without OJ is like a day without sunshine."
SemanticMetaphor
A word/phrase used imaginatively to describe something else — saying X IS Y with NO comparison word. Shows the two things share qualities.
"Your skin's new best friend." · "One touch and she realized freedom was something you feel."
SemanticMetonymy
Substitution of an attribute/adjunct for the whole thing — one characteristic stands for the entire entity.
"The press" = news media · "The Crown" = British monarchy · "I like Volvo" = I like Volvo cars
SemanticHyperbole
Deliberate overstatement or exaggeration to achieve emphasis. Very common in advertising superlatives.
"The best just got bigger!" · "So good, it should be illegal." · "No other pain-relieving gel works like this."
SemanticAntithesis
Uses the SAME or SIMILAR structure to express TWO OPPOSITE ideas. Creates contrast, memorability, and sharp rhythm. Based on antonyms or opposite ideas.
"Talks inside. Shouts outside." · "Imagine a mini phone with maximum style."
SemanticPresupposition
Something assumed to be true without being stated — harder for readers to deny than a direct assertion.
"Why do leading beauty experts use Perfectil?" (assumes they do use it)
Newsworthiness — 12 news values
News valueNegativity
Negative events (damage, injury, death, conflict, war) are the basic news value. Deviance also qualifies.
Disasters, crime, political conflict
News valueRecency
The best news is something which has JUST happened. Daily cycle for press/TV; hourly for radio.
A murder is more newsworthy than the ongoing investigation.
News valueProximity
Geographical closeness enhances news value. A minor local accident beats a major overseas one for local coverage.
The carnival coming to your town vs. the same carnival 100 miles away.
News value ⭐Consonance
A story's compatibility with PRECONCEPTIONS about the social group or nation from which the news actors come. Editors have stereotypes about how groups behave. NOT the same as relevance.
Stereotypes about Latin American governments or the British royal family.
News valueUnambiguity
The clearer and more clear-cut the story, the more newsworthy. Ifs, buts, and maybes are minimal.
Clear-cut verdict > ambiguous trial outcome
News valueUnexpectedness
Unpredictable or rare events are more newsworthy than routine ones. Closely related: Novelty.
A "man bites dog" story beats "dog bites man".
News valueSuperlativeness
The biggest, most violent, most destructive gets covered — extremes attract coverage.
"The worst floods in 50 years." · "The most expensive divorce in history."
News valueRelevance
The effect of a story on the audience's OWN lives or its closeness to their experience. NOT the same as proximity (which is geographical).
A US policy change affecting Vietnamese exports is relevant in Vietnam even if geographically distant.
News valuePersonalization
Something that can be pictured in personal/individual terms is more newsworthy than a concept or a mass.
One identifiable victim > abstract statistics of thousands.
News valueEliteness
Reference to elite persons (politicians, film stars) or elite First World nations makes something more newsworthy.
A politician's scandal > an ordinary person's similar behaviour.
News valueAttribution
The eliteness and authority of a story's sources. Highly valued sources need to be socially validated — unaffiliated individuals are not well regarded.
Government spokesperson > anonymous source
News valueFacticity
The degree to which a story contains facts and figures: locations, names, sums of money, numbers of all kinds.
"A £4.2 billion deal signed in Tokyo on Tuesday."
Language features of TV news
Pronunciation3 characteristics
News readers' pronunciation must be: (1) CLEAR (2) UNDERSTANDABLE (3) STANDARD (prestige variety). Dialects that identify the speaker as an individual must be avoided.
StructureReduced complexity
Sentences are predominantly SHORT. Complex syntactic structures (hypotaxis, multiple sub-ordination) do not occur very often. Underlying reason: RECIPIENT DESIGN — listeners can't rewind, so cognitive load must stay low.
Max 20 words per sentence except in unusual circumstances.
StructureActive vs. passive voice
ACTIVE VOICE dominates (adds drama). Passive voice used occasionally for formality — putting the object first takes attention away from the subject.
StructureStructure-enhancing features
Appositional noun phrases + attributive adjectives used to CONDENSE INFORMATION — specifically to (1) refer to a source of reported information and (2) specify the identity of persons in the news.
"John Smith, the company's CEO and father of three, denied the claims."
Word choice3 aspects of objectivity
(1) Absence of COLLOQUIAL language (2) Absence of AFFECTIVE language (anger, despair, grief) (3) Absence of PERSONAL VIEWPOINTS and opinions. All three reduce subjectivity.
No "this shocking tragedy" — just "the accident"
Word choiceSynonyms
Near-synonyms are used to keep listeners INTERESTED and avoid boring repetition. If the same word is repeated more than once or twice, listeners may switch off.
"yacht" and "boat" used alternately in the same story
Word choiceUnfamiliar words — placement
An unfamiliar word or name must NEVER be put FIRST in a paragraph. Work toward it over familiar ground — approach it from a known context first.
Wrong: "Myocarditis was diagnosed in the patient." Right: "The patient was diagnosed with heart inflammation — myocarditis."
Word choiceKey word repetition
Important words should be REPEATED at least once. Listeners may tune in mid-bulletin and cannot go back — repetition ensures key facts are retained.
TensePresent tense preferred
Present tense (or the tense NEAREST to the present, including continuous) is preferred. Emphasises IMMEDIACY — shows the event is current or still happening. Never waste broadcasting's advantage of immediacy.
"The President is meeting cabinet ministers." NOT "The President met cabinet ministers."
OtherTabloidization
The shift of news toward entertainment. One outcome: EMOTIONALIZATION — linguistic strategies to emotionally move and entertain the audience (colloquial language, affective lexicon). NOT desired in public service news.
OtherRecipient design
The underlying reason for reduced structural complexity. Spoken TV news is designed around the receiver's cognitive limitations — listeners cannot re-read, so simple structures are essential for information retention.
5 interview types
InterviewInformation interview
Aim: find out FACTS AND FIGURES about a topic. Interviewee's personality is not relevant — only their expertise. Interviewees: usually EXPERTS, POLITICIANS, or people directly involved in the topic. Broadcast as a whole.
Interviewing an environmental minister about emissions quotas.
InterviewOpinion interview
Aim: find out the VIEWPOINT of the interviewee on a specific issue, event, or development. Broadcast as a whole.
Asking a company chairman about protests against his factory.
InterviewPersonality interview (portrait)
Aim: reveal the LIFE, CHARACTER and EXPERIENCES of the interviewee. Questions mainly deal with personality. Broadcast as a whole.
"How did you get so involved in this cause?" — focuses on the person, not just the issue.
InterviewResearch interview
Aim: double-check information or research for a report/news item. Usually NOT broadcast. Used as background research by the journalist.
Confirming how much a company invested in environmental protection before writing a report.
Interview ⭐Statement interview
Aim: yield an INDIVIDUAL STATEMENT (sound bite) that becomes part of another journalistic piece. It is an interview used as a TOOL — NOT broadcast as a whole, only the clip is used.
Recording a short quote to insert into a news report.
Question types
ClosedYes/No question
Only allows "yes", "no", or "I don't know". Good to PINPOINT an interviewee on a specific topic or get a definite commitment. Used at the END of the funnel structure. NOT for getting the interviewee talking.
"Is this true?" "Do you believe PILLCO will fulfil the contract?"
ClosedInformation question
Closed question for SHORT, PRECISE information. Good as a follow-up or to specify an issue.
"How many people will be rehoused?" → "Around 2,000."
OpenOpen question
Answers are longer — gives the interviewee space to explain, elaborate, give background and personal opinions. Usually starts with What/Why/How.
"Why is it essential for the factory to be built here?"
SpecialStatement + question
Interviewer states a FACT and then adds a question. RISK: if the fact is wrong or unclear, the interviewee argues about the statement instead of answering.
"Most people oppose this project. Why haven't you moved the location?"
SpecialSuggestive question
Implies something, hoping the interviewee will react vividly. Forces the interviewee to take a stance. RISK: can be seen as provocation, damaging the interview atmosphere.
"So you're ready to accept that thousands of people will be uprooted?"
SpecialInterpreting question
Offers a possible INTERPRETATION of a vague or evasive answer. Forces the interviewee to confirm or deny, making their position clear for listeners.
"So that means the factory could not have been built anywhere else in town?"
Special ⭐Indirect question
Confronts the interviewee with a statement made by a THIRD PARTY. Allows the interviewer to be confrontational WITHOUT damaging the interview atmosphere — you "hide behind" the third party's remarks.
"Environmental groups say the factory will cause serious air pollution. What is your response?"
Badly-worded ✗Either/or question
AVOID: narrows the interviewee to ONLY TWO OPTIONS. They may have a third option you did not consider. Usually requires wasteful follow-up questions.
"Are you against this in principle, or are you primarily concerned about the environment?"
Badly-worded ✗Multiple question
AVOID: asking TWO OR MORE questions at once. The interviewee picks which part to answer, and you lose control. Always ask ONE question at a time.
"How many jobs will this create and what will it mean for the economy?"
Interview structure
StructureFunnel structure
Start with BROAD/GENERAL open questions → progressively get more SPECIFIC → end with a YES/NO question that pinpoints the interviewee on a clear statement. Leaves listeners with clear, concise information.
StructureLead question
The overall information goal of the interview. May not be asked directly, but every question should serve to answer it. Defined during preparation.
"How will PILLCO fulfil the environmental protection measures laid down in the contract?"
